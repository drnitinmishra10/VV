E20 Ethanol Blending in India: Does It Really Save Fuel Imports?

Author
Dr. Nitin Mishra
Professor, Leading University of India

Abstract

India has introduced the E20 fuel policy, which mandates blending 20% ethanol with petrol to reduce crude oil imports and improve energy security. While the policy appears attractive on paper, several economic and agricultural concerns deserve careful examination. If ethanol blending significantly reduces vehicle mileage, the expected reduction in petrol imports may become negligible. Moreover, producing ethanol from food grains raises important concerns regarding food security, water consumption, agricultural sustainability, and overall economic efficiency. This article critically evaluates whether E20 blending genuinely benefits India.

1. Introduction

India imports nearly 85% of its crude oil requirement, making petroleum imports one of the largest contributors to the country's trade deficit. To reduce dependence on imported crude oil, the Government of India has accelerated ethanol blending under the National Biofuel Policy.

The primary objective of E20 fuel is to replace 20% of petrol with domestically produced ethanol. However, the success of this policy depends on two important assumptions:

  • The reduction in vehicle mileage remains minimal.
  • Ethanol production does not adversely affect food production.

If either assumption fails, the economic benefits become questionable.

2. Does E20 Actually Reduce Petrol Imports?

The answer depends on how much fuel efficiency decreases after ethanol blending.

Illustrative Example

Parameter Pure Petrol E20 (20% Mileage Reduction)
Vehicle Mileage 20 km/litre 16 km/litre
Fuel Required for 100 km 5.0 litres 6.25 litres
Petrol Used 5.0 litres 5.0 litres
Ethanol Used 0 litre 1.25 litres
Although 20% of the fuel is ethanol, the vehicle consumes more total fuel because of lower mileage. If mileage actually decreases by nearly 20%, petrol consumption remains almost unchanged. In such a scenario, the expected reduction in crude oil imports becomes negligible.

3. Energy Density of Ethanol

Ethanol contains substantially less energy than petrol.

Fuel Energy Content (MJ/Litre)
Petrol ≈34.2
Ethanol ≈21.1

Since ethanol possesses approximately one-third lower energy density than petrol, vehicles generally require a larger quantity of blended fuel to travel the same distance unless engines are specifically optimized for higher ethanol blends.

4. Ethanol Production Requires Agricultural Resources

India currently produces ethanol primarily from:

  • Sugarcane
  • Maize (Corn)
  • Broken Rice
  • Damaged Food Grains

These feedstocks require substantial amounts of:

  • Fertile agricultural land
  • Irrigation water
  • Fertilizers
  • Electricity
  • Diesel-powered farm machinery
  • Transportation infrastructure

Consequently, ethanol production itself consumes considerable energy and natural resources before it reaches fuel stations.

5. Impact on Food Security

One of the strongest criticisms of food-based ethanol is its direct competition with food production.

  • Food grains diverted to ethanol are no longer available for human consumption.
  • Livestock feed becomes more expensive.
  • Poultry production costs increase.
  • Milk, eggs, and chicken prices may rise.
  • Government food procurement becomes more difficult.
  • Pressure increases on agricultural land.
Using food crops as transportation fuel may shift agricultural priorities from feeding people to fueling vehicles, creating long-term food security challenges for a country with a large population.

6. Water Consumption

Sugarcane, one of the primary ethanol feedstocks in India, is among the world's most water-intensive crops.

Large-scale expansion of sugarcane cultivation may accelerate groundwater depletion in several states including:

  • Maharashtra
  • Karnataka
  • Punjab
  • Haryana

In a country already facing severe water stress, increasing water-intensive fuel crops raises significant sustainability concerns.

7. Economic Trade-offs

Although ethanol blending may reduce crude oil imports, India could simultaneously increase imports of:

  • Edible oils
  • Animal feed
  • Maize
  • Agricultural fertilizers

Therefore, apparent savings in petroleum imports may be offset by increased imports in other sectors.

8. Environmental Considerations

Ethanol is frequently described as a cleaner fuel. However, its environmental benefits depend heavily on production methods.

Lifecycle emissions increase due to:

  • Heavy fertilizer usage
  • Diesel-operated agricultural machinery
  • Energy-intensive irrigation
  • Transportation of biomass
  • Industrial processing of ethanol

Therefore, ethanol cannot automatically be considered carbon neutral.

9. Better Alternatives

A more sustainable long-term strategy would prioritize second-generation (2G) ethanol produced from agricultural residues rather than food crops.

Examples include:

  • Rice straw
  • Sugarcane bagasse
  • Crop residues
  • Municipal organic waste
  • Agricultural biomass

Such feedstocks avoid direct competition with food production while simultaneously reducing agricultural waste.

10. Balanced Assessment

If E20 fuel reduces vehicle mileage by only 3–8%, as observed in many modern E20-compatible vehicles, ethanol blending can contribute to moderate reductions in petrol imports while improving energy diversification.

However, if mileage reduction approaches 15–20%, the expected savings in imported petrol become significantly smaller because more blended fuel is required to travel the same distance.

Furthermore, producing ethanol from food grains places additional pressure on agriculture, groundwater resources, fertilizer demand, and food markets.

Conclusion

India's objective of reducing crude oil imports is strategically important. Nevertheless, the long-term success of ethanol blending depends on balancing energy security with food security. If substantial quantities of maize, rice, and sugarcane are diverted toward fuel production while vehicle fuel efficiency declines considerably, the overall economic advantage becomes questionable. A more sustainable national strategy would prioritize second-generation ethanol derived from agricultural waste, crop residues, and biomass rather than edible food grains. Such an approach would reduce dependence on imported petroleum without compromising food availability, water resources, or agricultural sustainability.
